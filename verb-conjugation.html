Present Tense Conjugation Das Präsens

A1

⚡ Key Points

  • German Präsens covers both present and near-future meaning — "Ich lerne" = "I learn" and "I am learning" and "I will learn (tomorrow)".
  • Conjugation = verb stem (infinitive minus -en or -n) + personal ending.
  • The six endings for regular verbs: -e, -st, -t, -en, -t, -en (ich/du/er/wir/ihr/sie).
  • Strong verbs change their stem vowel only in 2nd and 3rd person singular (du / er-sie-es).
  • The three patterns of vowel change: e → i (geben), e → ie (lesen), a → ä (fahren).
  • sein, haben, werden are fully irregular — memorise them as top priority.
  • Modal verbs have no ending in 1st and 3rd person singular: ich kann, er kann.
  • Separable verbs split: the prefix moves to the end of the main clause.

3. The Big Three — sein, haben, werden

These three verbs are fully irregular and are used constantly — as main verbs and as auxiliaries for perfect tense and passive voice. Memorise them completely.

Sein / Haben / Werden in Use

Ich bin müde. — I am tired.

Er hat Hunger. — He is hungry. (literally: has hunger)

Sie wird Ärztin. — She is becoming a doctor.

Wir haben das Buch gelesen. — We have read the book. (Perfekt auxiliary)

4. Modal Verbs (Modalverben)

Modal verbs express ability, permission, necessity, desire, or obligation. They have two important quirks: (1) no ending in ich and er/sie/es, and (2) they push the main verb as an infinitive to the end of the clause.

Modals + Infinitive at the End

Ich kann gut Deutsch sprechen. — I can speak German well.

Du musst jetzt lernen. — You must study now.

Er will ein Buch kaufen. — He wants to buy a book.

Wir dürfen hier nicht rauchen. — We are not allowed to smoke here.

🔑 möchten — the polite form: At A1/A2 level, you'll use möchten ("would like to") more than mögen. It's technically Konjunktiv II of mögen but behaves like a modal: Ich möchte einen Kaffee. (I would like a coffee.) It follows regular weak-verb endings, including er möchte (not möchtet).

5. Separable Verbs (Trennbare Verben)

Separable verbs consist of a prefix (often a preposition or adverb) + a base verb. When conjugated in a main clause, the prefix detaches and moves to the very end of the clause, while the conjugated verb stays in position 2.

Infinitive Meaning Conjugated Example
aufmachento openIch mache die Tür auf. (I open the door.)
anrufento call (phone)Er ruft seine Mutter an. (He calls his mother.)
abfahrento departDer Zug fährt um 9 Uhr ab. (The train departs at 9.)
einkaufento shopWir kaufen heute ein. (We're shopping today.)
zurückkommento come backSie kommt morgen zurück. (She comes back tomorrow.)
⚠️ Inseparable prefixes — these verbs do NOT split: Verbs beginning with be-, ge-, er-, ver-, zer-, ent-, emp-, miss- are inseparable. verstehen (to understand) → ich verstehe (not ich stehe ver). The stress in speech is on the BASE verb in separable verbs (AUFmachen) and on the BASE also in inseparables (verSTEHen).

🔗 Connected Topics

  • German Cases The nominative subject must agree with the verb — cases identify the subject that drives conjugation.
  • Sentence Structure The conjugated verb always takes position 2 in main clauses; modals and separable verbs create the verbal bracket (Satzklammer).
  • Noun Gender Third-person pronouns (er/sie/es) are assigned by grammatical gender, so choosing the right subject pronoun depends on gender.
  • Article Declension The subject (nominative article + noun) must agree with the verb — articles and conjugation connect at the subject.
