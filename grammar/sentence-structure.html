Sentence Structure & Word Order Satzstruktur & Wortstellung

A1 / A2

⚡ Key Points

  • The finite verb always occupies position 2 in a main clause — this is the most important rule in German syntax.
  • Position 1 can be the subject, a time expression, a place, an object, or any other element — but position 2 is always the verb.
  • German can move almost any element to position 1 for emphasis — cases, not word order, signal who is the subject/object.
  • In subordinate clauses (after weil, dass, wenn, obwohl…), the finite verb moves to the very end.
  • Modal verbs and auxiliary verbs create a verbal bracket (Satzklammer): conjugated verb in position 2, infinitive or participle at the end.
  • Adverbs follow the order TeKaMoLo: Temporal → Causal → Modal → Local.
  • For yes/no questions, the verb moves to position 1 (subject follows).
  • For W-questions, the question word takes position 1, verb stays in position 2.

The Verb-Second (V2) Rule

In English, the subject almost always comes first: "Yesterday I studied German." German allows remarkable flexibility — any element can open the sentence — but no matter what is in position 1, the finite (conjugated) verb must immediately follow it in position 2.

V2 Rule — Same Sentence, Different Emphasis

Ich lerne heute Deutsch. — I'm learning German today. (neutral, subject first)

Heute lerne ich Deutsch. — Today I'm learning German. (time element emphasised)

Deutsch lerne ich heute. — German is what I'm learning today. (object emphasised)

In every version: lerne = position 2 ✓ · ich shifts around but the verb stays fixed.

🔑 The inversion rule: When anything other than the subject is in position 1, the subject and verb swap: the verb moves to position 2 and the subject immediately follows it. This is called subject-verb inversion (Inversion). It is not optional — the V2 rule is obligatory in main clauses.

The Verbal Bracket (Satzklammer)

When a sentence uses a modal verb, auxiliary verb, or separable verb prefix, the main clause creates a bracket: the finite verb holds position 2 and the non-finite element (infinitive, past participle, or separable prefix) is sent to the very end of the clause. Everything else — objects, adverbs, negation — sits inside the bracket.

Satzklammer Visualised

Ich [kann] heute keine Zeit [finden]. — I can't find time today.

Er [hat] das Buch gestern schon [gelesen]. — He already read the book yesterday.

Wir [machen] die Fenster immer abends [zu]. — We always close the windows in the evenings. (separable: zumachen)

Sentence Type Position 2 (finite verb) Sentence End (non-finite)
Modal verb kann / muss / will… Infinitive of main verb
Perfekt (present perfect) habe / ist… (auxiliary) Past participle (gemacht, gegangen)
Futur I (future) werde / wird… Infinitive
Separable verb Conjugated base verb (mache, rufe) Prefix (auf, an, ab, zu…)

Adverb Ordering — Te-Ka-Mo-Lo

When a German sentence contains multiple adverbial elements, they appear in a fixed preferred order inside the verbal bracket: Temporal (when) → Kausal (why) → Modal (how) → Lokal (where). This is the TeKaMoLo rule.

Letter Type Question Examples
Te Temporal Wann? Wie lange? Wie oft? heute, gestern, um 9 Uhr, immer, oft
Ka Kausal Warum? Aus welchem Grund? wegen des Wetters, aus Neugier, deshalb
Mo Modal Wie? Womit? Mit wem? mit dem Bus, alleine, gut, schnell, mit meiner Schwester
Lo Lokal Wo? Wohin? Woher? in Berlin, nach Hause, aus der Schule, hier
TeKaMoLo in a Full Sentence

Ich fahre [heute] [wegen der Arbeit] [mit dem Zug] [nach München].

→ Temporal: heute · Kausal: wegen der Arbeit · Modal: mit dem Zug · Lokal: nach München

Sie hat [gestern] [mit Freunden] [in der Stadt] gegessen. — She ate with friends in the city yesterday.

🔑 TeKaMoLo is a default, not a law: You can deviate for emphasis by placing an element in position 1. But within the bracket, this order reads most naturally to native ears and avoids sounding awkward.

Forming Questions

Yes/No Questions (Ja-/Nein-Fragen)

Move the finite verb to position 1 (before the subject). The subject immediately follows in position 2.

Yes/No Question Examples

Du lernst Deutsch. → Lernst du Deutsch? — Are you learning German?

Er kann schwimmen. → Kann er schwimmen? — Can he swim?

Das Wetter ist schön. → Ist das Wetter schön? — Is the weather nice?

W-Questions (W-Fragen)

The question word (W-word) takes position 1, and the finite verb stays in position 2 — exactly the same V2 rule applies.

W-Word Meaning Case Example
werwhoNominativeWer kommt heute? (Who is coming today?)
wenwhomAccusativeWen siehst du? (Whom do you see?)
wemto whomDativeWem gibst du das? (To whom do you give that?)
waswhatNom./Acc.Was machst du? (What are you doing?)
wowhere—Wo wohnst du? (Where do you live?)
wohinwhere to—Wohin fährst du? (Where are you going?)
wannwhen—Wann kommst du? (When are you coming?)
wiehow—Wie heißt du? (What's your name?)
warumwhy—Warum lernst du Deutsch? (Why are you learning German?)
wie viel(e)how much/many—Wie viel kostet das? (How much does that cost?)

Subordinate Clauses (Nebensätze) — Verb at the End

When a subordinating conjunction introduces a clause, the entire conjugated verb moves to the absolute end of that clause. If there is a verbal bracket (modal + infinitive), both the auxiliary and the non-finite part sit at the end, with the finite verb last.

Conjunction Meaning Example
weil because Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Berlin wohne.
dass that Ich glaube, dass er Recht hat.
wenn when / if (cond.) Ruf mich an, wenn du nach Hause kommst.
obwohl although Er geht spazieren, obwohl es regnet.
als when (past, once) Als ich jung war, spielte ich Fußball.
ob whether / if Ich weiß nicht, ob er kommt.
damit so that Ich spreche laut, damit du mich verstehst.
Modal in a Subordinate Clause

Main clause: Ich kann heute nicht kommen. — I can't come today.

As subordinate: Er sagt, dass ich heute nicht kommen kann. — He says that I can't come today.

→ In the subordinate clause, infinitive (kommen) comes first, then the finite modal (kann) at the very end.

🔑 Subordinate → main clause inversion: When a subordinate clause begins the sentence (position 1), the main clause verb immediately follows (position 2 of the whole sentence), producing inversion: Weil ich müde bin, gehe ich schlafen. The entire subordinate clause counts as one element in position 1.

Negation Position — Where Does nicht Go?

Nicht generally appears before the element it negates. When negating the whole predicate, it sits just before the non-finite verb at the end of the bracket (or at the very end in simple clauses).

Nicht Position Examples

Er kommt nicht. — He is not coming. (negates entire predicate)

Ich kann nicht kommen. — I can't come. (before infinitive in bracket)

Das ist nicht gut. — That is not good. (before predicate adjective)

Ich komme nicht heute, sondern morgen. — I'm not coming today, but tomorrow. (negates just "today")

Summary — Main Clause vs. Subordinate Clause

Feature Main Clause (Hauptsatz) Subordinate Clause (Nebensatz)
Finite verb position Position 2 (always) Final position (last)
Conjunction Coordinating: und, oder, aber, denn, sondern Subordinating: weil, dass, wenn, obwohl, ob…
Conjunction effect on verb No change (verb stays in pos. 2) Verb moves to end
Modal / auxiliary Modal at pos. 2, infinitive at end (bracket) Infinitive then modal both at end
Example Ich lerne Deutsch. …weil ich Deutsch lerne.

  • Verb Conjugation The finite verb that occupies position 2 must be correctly conjugated — conjugation and word order rules are inseparable.
  • Article Declension Article forms (den vs. der vs. dem) identify case when word order shifts — declension enables flexible structure.

  • Adjective Declension Adjectives in predicate position (after sein) are uninflected; in attributive position, their endings also follow case and position.

✏️ Übungen (Exercises)

These exercises follow Goethe-Zertifikat A2 and TELC Deutsch A2 formats. Word order errors are among the most common mistakes — these drills target the V2 rule, subordinate clauses, and the verbal bracket directly.

Exercise 1 Lückentext Goethe A2 · Schreiben style

Conjugate the verb in brackets into the correct form for that clause type (main clause, question, subordinate clause, or modal bracket).

  1. Heute (lernen) ich Deutsch. (main clause — inversion after 'Heute')
  2. (hören) du gerne Musik? (yes/no question — verb in position 1)
  3. Er sagt, dass er Deutsch . (lernen) (subordinate clause after 'dass' — verb to end)
  4. Ich kann heute nicht . (kommen) (modal bracket — infinitive at end)
  5. Weil ich müde , gehe ich schlafen. (sein) (subordinate clause — finite verb to end)
Exercise 2 Sprachbausteine TELC A2 · Lesen Teil 5 style

Choose the correct subordinating conjunction to match the meaning of each sentence.

Ich lerne Deutsch, 1 ich in Berlin wohne. Er bleibt zu Hause, 2 er krank ist. 3 du nach Hause kommst, ruf mich an.

Hint: 1 = "because" (reason) · 2 = "although" (contrast) · 3 = "when/if" (repeated/future condition — not a past event)

Exercise 3 Multiple Choice Goethe A2 · Grammatik style

Identify the rule that explains the verb's position in each sentence.

  • …weil er heute keine Zeit hat. — Why is 'hat' at the end?

  • Heute lerne ich Deutsch. — Why does 'lerne' come before 'ich'?

  • Kann er schwimmen? — Why is 'kann' in position 1?

  • Ich muss jetzt nach Hause gehen. — Why is 'gehen' at the end?

Exercise 4 Fehlerkorrektur TELC A2 · Grammatik style

Each subordinate clause has a wrong verb form (in red) for the person shown. Type the correct conjugated form.

  • Sie sagt, dass sie Deutsch lernst. Correct →
  • Ich hoffe, dass du morgen kommen. Correct →
  • Weil ich müde bist, gehe ich schlafen. Correct →
  • Er fragt, ob wir morgen kommt. Correct →
