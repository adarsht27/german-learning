German Adjective Suffixes Die Adjektivsuffixe

A2 / B1

⚡ Key Points

  • Adjective suffixes let you build new adjectives from nouns and verb stems — knowing 10 suffixes unlocks hundreds of words.
  • -isch ≈ -ic/-ical — attaches to loanwords and nationalities: elektrisch, romantisch, deutsch.
  • -bar ≈ -able/-ible — attaches to verb stems, means "can be done": essbar, trinkbar, machbar.
  • -haft = "having a quality" — often negative/excessive: fehlerhaft, ekelhaft, rätselhaft.
  • -sam ≈ English -some — innate tendency or disposition: langsam, sparsam, einsam.
  • -fest ≈ -proof/-resistant — attaches to nouns, stands firm against: wasserfest, hitzefest, feuerfest.
  • -voll = full of (emotional): liebevoll, humorvoll. -reich = rich in (quantitative): erfolgreich, abwechslungsreich.
  • -frei = deliberately without (positive, product labels): alkoholfrei, glutenfrei. -los = lacking something (everyday): arbeitslos, hilflos. -leer = empty of (literary): menschenleer.
  • The suffix itself never changes; only the declension ending added after it varies by gender and case.

What Are Adjective Suffixes?

German builds adjectives systematically by attaching fixed endings — suffixes — to nouns and verb stems. Once you know the suffix, you can often guess the meaning of an unfamiliar adjective immediately. For a beginner this is one of the highest-value patterns to learn: instead of memorising isolated words, you gain a production rule that works across the entire vocabulary.

The suffix itself does not change with gender or case. What changes is the declension ending added after it: ein elektrischer Zug (masc. nom. indef.) but der elektrische Zug (masc. nom. def.). The -isch part stays fixed in both.

1. -isch  ≈ English -ic / -ical

🔑 Nationality adjectives also use -isch: türkisch (Turkish), japanisch (Japanese), englisch (English). Note: nationality adjectives are not capitalised in German — unlike in English.

2. -bar  ≈ English -able / -ible

Formation: Attaches to verb stems. Remove the infinitive ending -en and add -bar. Meaning: "can be [verb]ed." Example: essen → ess + bar = essbar (edible).

🔑 Memory hook: Think of "bearable" — something you can bear/handle. -bar asks: can this verb be done to it?
⚠️ -bar vs. -lich: Both can translate as -able/-ible but attach differently. -bar attaches to verb stems and means "can be done" (essbar, hörbar). -lich attaches to nouns/adjectives meaning "having the character of" (persönlich, freundlich, mündlich).

Formation: Both attach to nouns. voll = full; reich = rich. They overlap in meaning but are not interchangeable.

🔑 Key distinction — -voll vs. -reich:

-voll describes emotional or qualitative fullness — feelings, atmosphere, character traits. (liebevoll, hoffnungsvoll, humorvoll, sinnvoll)

-reich describes quantitative abundance — having many of something you could in theory count. (abwechslungsreich, erfahrungsreich, ideenreich)

Rule of thumb: emotions / how someone treats others → -voll. Variety or abundance of something tangible → -reich. Exception: erfolgreich (successful) must simply be memorised.

🔑 The Big Three — when to use which:

-frei → deliberately without something — a desirable, marketed feature. Language of product labels. (alkoholfrei, glutenfrei, stressfrei)

-los → lacking something — often negative. Person or situation without something it needs. Most common in everyday speech. (arbeitslos, hilflos, sinnlos)

-leer → empty of something — literary and poetic, implies a hollow void. Rare in casual speech. (menschenleer, inhaltsleer)
🔑 Register note: hoffnungslos (everyday, use in speech and writing) vs. hoffnungsleer (literary, poetic). The -leer vs. -los distinction reliably maps to written/poetic vs. spoken/everyday.

🔗 Connected Topics

  • Noun Gender Most suffix-derived adjectives come from nouns — knowing the gender of the root noun helps you understand the word's origin.
  • Article Declension Once you form an adjective with a suffix, you need to add the correct declension ending — that depends on case, gender, and article type.
  • The Four German Cases Adjective endings (-e, -en, -em, -er, -es) change with case. Cases are the foundation for using any adjective correctly in a sentence.
  • Verb Conjugation The suffix -bar attaches directly to verb stems — understanding verb infinitives makes -bar adjectives easy to form and recognise.
  • Sentence Structure Adjectives appear as attributive (before the noun) or predicative (after sein/werden). Position affects whether a declension ending is needed.
