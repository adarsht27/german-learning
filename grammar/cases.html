The Four German Cases Die vier deutschen Fälle

A1 / A2

⚡ Key Points

  • German has four cases: Nominative, Accusative, Dative, and Genitive — each signals a noun's role in the sentence.
  • Unlike English, German uses case endings, not word order, to show who is doing what to whom.
  • Nominative = the subject (who/what performs the action).
  • Accusative = the direct object (who/what is directly acted upon).
  • Dative = the indirect object (to/for whom the action is done).
  • Genitive = possession (whose / of what).
  • Only masculine articles change between Nominative and Accusative (der → den); feminine and neuter stay the same.
  • Certain prepositions always lock a noun into a specific case — memorise these lists.
  • Cases affect articles, adjective endings, and pronouns — this is why cases matter for everything.

What Are Cases?

In English, the words "I", "me", and "my" refer to the same person but change depending on how that person relates to the sentence. German applies this same idea far more systematically — not just to pronouns, but to every noun phrase, including its article and any adjective attached to it.

Cases allow German to use flexible word order for emphasis while keeping meaning unambiguous. Consider: Den Mann beißt der Hund — despite Mann coming first, the accusative article den marks him as the object (the dog bites the man), not the subject. A learner who understands cases can read any sentence correctly, no matter how scrambled.

1. Nominativ — The Nominative Case

The nominative is the subject case. The subject is the person or thing performing the verb's action. Every German sentence has a subject, and that subject is always in the nominative.

Question to identify it: Wer? / Was? (Who? / What? — as the doer)

Nominative Examples

Der Mann kauft Brot. — The man buys bread. (Der Mann = nom. masc.)

Die Frau liest ein Buch. — The woman reads a book. (Die Frau = nom. fem.)

Das Kind schläft. — The child sleeps. (Das Kind = nom. neut.)

Die Hunde bellen laut. — The dogs bark loudly. (Die Hunde = nom. pl.)

Ich bin müde. — I am tired. (Pronoun in nominative.)

🔑 Nominative after sein, werden, bleiben: These "linking verbs" connect two nominative phrases — both subject and predicate noun are nominative. Er ist ein Arzt (He is a doctor) — Arzt stays nominative, not accusative.

2. Akkusativ — The Accusative Case

The accusative marks the direct object — the noun directly receiving the action. It is also required after a fixed set of prepositions.

Question to identify it: Wen? / Was? (Whom? / What? — as the receiver)

The good news: only masculine articles change in the accusative. Feminine, neuter, and plural forms are identical to the nominative.

Accusative Examples

Ich kaufe den Apfel. — I buy the apple. (den = acc. masc., cf. nom. der)

Sie liebt einen Mann. — She loves a man. (einen = acc. masc. indef.)

Er sieht die Frau. — He sees the woman. (fem. unchanged)

Wir haben das Auto. — We have the car. (neut. unchanged)

Ich kenne die Kinder. — I know the children. (pl. unchanged)

🔑 Accusative prepositions (always):
durch (through) · für (for) · gegen (against) · ohne (without) · um (around / at) · bis (until/to) · entlang (along — follows the noun)

Memory hook: D-F-G-O-U — durch, für, gegen, ohne, um are the core five.

3. Dativ — The Dative Case

The dative marks the indirect object — typically the recipient or beneficiary of an action. If you can say "to whom" or "for whom" in English, that noun is in the dative. The dative also follows a specific set of prepositions and certain verbs.

Question to identify it: Wem? (To whom? / For whom?)

Dative Examples

Ich gebe dem Mann das Buch. — I give the man the book. (dem = dat. masc.)

Sie hilft der Frau. — She helps the woman. (der = dat. fem.)

Das gehört dem Kind. — That belongs to the child. (dem = dat. neut.)

Er schreibt den Freunden. — He writes to the friends. (den + noun gets -n in dat. pl.)

Ich wohne bei meiner Mutter. — I live at my mother's. (bei is a dative preposition)

🔑 Dative prepositions (always):
aus (out of) · bei (at/near) · mit (with) · nach (after/to) · seit (since/for) · von (from/by) · zu (to/at) · außer (except) · gegenüber (opposite)

Memory rhyme: "aus bei mit nach seit von zu" — say it like a chant until it sticks.
🔑 Verbs that always take dative: helfen (to help), danken (to thank), gefallen (to please/like), gehören (to belong to), folgen (to follow), glauben (to believe someone). These don't have a direct object — the person is always dative.

4. Genitiv — The Genitive Case

The genitive expresses possession, belonging, or relationship between two nouns — equivalent to English "'s" or "of." It also follows certain prepositions. In everyday spoken German, von + dative often replaces the genitive, but you'll encounter it constantly in writing, formal speech, and B1+ material.

Question to identify it: Wessen? (Whose?)

In the genitive, masculine and neuter nouns add -s (or -es for one-syllable words); feminine and plural nouns are unchanged.

Genitive Examples

Das Auto des Mannes. — The man's car. (des + -es = gen. masc.)

Die Tasche der Frau. — The woman's bag. (der = gen. fem.)

Der Name des Kindes. — The child's name. (des + -es = gen. neut.)

Das Haus der Eltern. — The parents' house. (der = gen. pl.)

Wegen des Wetters bleibe ich zu Hause. — Because of the weather I stay home. (wegen = gen. preposition)

🔑 Genitive prepositions (common):
wegen (because of) · trotz (despite) · während (during) · statt / anstatt (instead of) · innerhalb (inside of) · außerhalb (outside of) · aufgrund (due to)
⚠️ Spoken vs. written German: In casual speech, Germans often say wegen dem Wetter (dative) rather than wegen des Wetters (genitive). Both exist, but the genitive form is expected in writing and formal contexts.

Two-Way Prepositions (Wechselpräpositionen)

Nine prepositions take accusative when expressing movement/direction toward a destination, and dative when expressing static location (where something is).

Preposition Dative — Location (Wo?) Accusative — Direction (Wohin?)
an (at/on) Das Bild hängt an der Wand. Ich hänge das Bild an die Wand.
auf (on/onto) Das Buch liegt auf dem Tisch. Ich lege das Buch auf den Tisch.
in (in/into) Ich bin in der Schule. Ich gehe in die Schule.
über, unter, vor, hinter, neben, zwischen Same pattern: sein / liegen / stehen / hängen → dative; legen / stellen / hängen → accusative
🔑 Quick test for two-way prepositions: Ask Wo? (where?) → dative. Ask Wohin? (where to?) → accusative. Das Buch liegt auf dem Tisch (Wo? → dative). Ich lege das Buch auf den Tisch (Wohin? → accusative).

🔗 Connected Topics

  • Article Declension How der / die / das and ein / eine change through all four cases — the direct application of case theory.
  • Noun Gender Grammatical gender (der / die / das) must be known before you can apply the right case article — they are inseparable.
  • Present Tense Verb Conjugation The subject (nominative noun) must agree with the verb — conjugation and cases work together in every sentence.
  • Sentence Structure & Word Order Cases are why German allows flexible word order — position doesn't determine subject/object; case endings do.
  • Adjective Declension Adjectives take endings that mirror the case and gender of their noun — this follows directly from mastering cases.
