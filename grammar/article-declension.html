Article Declension Die Artikeldeklination

A1 / A2

⚡ Key Points

  • German articles change form based on the noun's gender, case, and number.
  • There are four article types: definite (the), indefinite (a/an), negative (no/not a), and possessive (my/your/his…).
  • Only masculine articles change between Nominative and Accusative (der → den, ein → einen).
  • Indefinite, negative, and possessive articles all follow the same pattern — learn ein and you know them all.
  • Possessives take the same endings as kein — this is called the ein-word pattern.
  • The null article (no article) is used for professions, nationalities, and materials in certain constructions.
  • Feminine dative (der) looks like masculine nominative — context and verb agreement resolve any ambiguity.
  • Memorising both tables (definite + indefinite) unlocks adjective declension as a natural next step.

Why Articles Change Form

German articles are not fixed words the way English "the" or "a" is. They carry grammatical information — specifically, the gender of the noun and its case (the role it plays in the sentence). Reading an article form tells you instantly whether a noun is the subject, direct object, indirect object, or possessor — without relying on word order.

There are two families of articles: the der-words (definite articles and a few related words) and the ein-words (indefinite, negative, and possessive articles). Master these two families and you have the foundation for adjective declension, pronoun forms, and relative clauses.

1. Definite Articles — der / die / das (the)

Use definite articles when the noun is specific and known to both speaker and listener — something already mentioned, unique, or identified by context.

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative der die das die
Accusative den ← die das die
Dative dem der dem den (+n)
Genitive des (+s) der des (+s) der
Definite Article in Context

Der Mann kauft das Brot für die Frau. — The man buys the bread for the woman.

→ Mann: nominative masc. → der · Brot: accusative neut. → das · Frau: dative fem. (after für? No — für is accusative. Dative because of für… actually: für = accusative → die Frau acc. fem. unchanged).

Corrected reading: Frau after für = accusative feminine = die (same as nominative). All three cases visible in one sentence.

🔑 The four forms that change most: der → den (masc. acc.), der → dem (masc. dat.), die → der (fem. dat.), die → den (+n) (pl. dat.). Everything else either stays the same or is predictable from these.

2. Indefinite Articles — ein / eine (a / an)

Use indefinite articles for unspecified or first-mentioned nouns. Indefinite articles have no plural form (German uses either no article, or einige / manche for "some").

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative ein eine ein — (no article)
Accusative einen ← eine ein — (no article)
Dative einem einer einem — / keinen
Genitive eines (+s) einer eines (+s) —
Indefinite Article Examples

Ich sehe einen Hund. — I see a dog. (acc. masc. → einen)

Sie hat eine Katze. — She has a cat. (acc. fem. → eine, unchanged)

Er gibt einem Kind ein Buch. — He gives a child a book. (dat. neut. → einem)

3. Negative Articles — kein / keine (no / not a)

Kein negates a noun directly and follows exactly the same pattern as ein, with the addition of plural forms. Use kein wherever you could use ein in the positive; use it for plural nouns without articles.

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative kein keine kein keine
Accusative keinen keine kein keine
Dative keinem keiner keinem keinen (+n)
Genitive keines keiner keines keiner
Kein vs. Nicht

Ich habe keinen Hund. — I have no dog. (negates the noun itself)

Ich habe nicht geschlafen. — I didn't sleep. (negates the verb or predicate)

Das ist kein Bier. — That's not beer. (negates a predicate noun)

🔑 kein vs. nicht: If you can insert "a / an" or "no" in English before the noun → use kein. If the negation is about the action or an adjective/adverb → use nicht.

4. Possessive Articles (Possessivartikel) — The Ein-Word Pattern

Possessive articles express ownership. They all follow the same endings as kein — once you know one, you know all of them. The stem changes per person; the endings are identical.

Person Stem English
1st sg.mein-my
2nd sg. (informal)dein-your
3rd sg. masc./neut.sein-his / its
3rd sg. fem.ihr-her
1st pl.unser-our
2nd pl. (informal)euer-your (pl.)
3rd pl.ihr-their
Formal (sg. & pl.)Ihr-your (formal)

Add the same endings as kein to any stem above:

Case Masc. (mein Bruder) Fem. (meine Schwester) Neut. (mein Kind) Plural (meine Eltern)
Nominative mein meine mein meine
Accusative meinen meine mein meine
Dative meinem meiner meinem meinen (+n)
Genitive meines meiner meines meiner
Possessive Article Examples

Das ist mein Bruder. — That is my brother. (nom. masc. → mein)

Ich vermisse meine Schwester. — I miss my sister. (acc. fem. → meine, unchanged)

Er fährt mit seinem Auto. — He drives with his car. (dat. neut. after mit → seinem)

Das ist das Zimmer unserer Kinder. — That is our children's room. (gen. pl. → unserer)

⚠️ euer drops the internal e when endings are added:
Nom. masc.: euer → Acc. masc.: euren (not eueren) · Dat. masc.: eurem · Fem. nom./acc.: eure

5. The Null Article — When to Use No Article

German omits the article in several regular situations. This is called the Nullartikel.

Context Example Note
Professions / roles after sein, werden, bleiben Sie ist Ärztin. Er wird Lehrer. No article unless an adjective is added: eine gute Ärztin
Nationalities / religions Ich bin Deutscher. Er ist Muslim. Same rule as professions
Uncountable nouns / materials Ich trinke Kaffee. Wir brauchen Holz. General substance, not a specific portion
Most proper nouns / country names Ich komme aus Deutschland. Exception: countries with article — die Schweiz, der Iran, die USA
Greetings / set phrases Guten Morgen! Auf Wiedersehen! Fixed expressions without article
